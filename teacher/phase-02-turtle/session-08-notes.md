## Session 8 — Teacher Notes

*Phase 2, Python with Turtle · Session 8 of 8 · Title: Milestone
project + demo day*

### Purpose of this session

Last day of Phase 2. Compresses Phase 1's two milestone sessions
(plan + build, then polish + demo) into a single session. Six
jobs, in priority order:

1. **Make sure every kid demos something.** Same rule as Phase 1
   Session 9 — no kid leaves without demoing, even if their
   project is trivial.
2. **Force planning, but quickly.** The Part A plan is shorter
   than Phase 1's (4 questions, not 7) because there's less
   time. Don't let kids skip it; do let them write briefly.
3. **Help every student scope realistically.** With only ~45
   minutes of build time, ambitious ideas WILL fail to finish.
   Push hard on "simplest version that's still cool."
4. **Run a clean demo half.** Same format as Phase 1. Specific
   praise, structured demos, one question per kid.
5. **End Phase 2 with a clear Phase 3 transition.** Phase 3 is
   a real shift — leaving the turtle, getting into general
   Python. Frame it positively as "going deeper" rather than
   "the visual fun is over."
6. **Note transition signals.** Watch for kids whose Phase 2
   experience suggests they'll need extra support in Phase 3 —
   typing speed, frustration tolerance, pace. Update your buddy
   pairings.

### Before class

**Bring:**

- Snacks if you can swing it (demo days deserve food).
- Class roster with two columns: project name + one specific
  praise note per kid.
- Optional: small "Phase 2 complete" recognition — sticker,
  certificate, whatever fits your culture.

**Set up:**

- Demo machine free for kids who'll demo on it.
- Each kid's project file accessible on the machine they'll
  demo on. If a kid has been working at home, pre-load before
  class.
- Chairs/spots arranged so everyone can see the demo machine
  during Part B.

**Prep time:** ~20 minutes. Review your Session 7 notes about
each kid's mentioned ideas. Walk in knowing where each kid
roughly is.

### Timing and flow

Total: ~90 min. **Tightest timing in either phase so far.**

- **Welcome and frame** (~5 min) — what today is, brief.
- **Part A: planning + building** (~50 min) — silent planning
  ~10 min, buddy + Mr. Eric review ~5 min (overlap), building
  ~35 min.
- **Break** (~5 min, can be skipped if behind).
- **Part B: polish + demos** (~30 min) — final polish ~5-10
  min, buddy swap ~3 min, demos at 3-4 min each (8 kids = 24-32
  min, push hard on time).

If you have ~6 kids, demos are 4-5 min each — comfortable. If
you have 8+, demos must be tight (3 min each) or the session
runs over. Plan accordingly.

If running over: **demos take priority over polish.** Cut the
polish window before cutting demo time.

### Teaching Part A

#### The frame

Open with the time pressure framing:

> "Today's tight. We have one session to plan, build, and demo.
> That means you have to make smart choices about what to build —
> not too ambitious. The most important question on the planning
> sheet is question four: what's the SIMPLEST version that's
> still cool? That's what you're building first."

This sets expectations. Kids who arrive with grandiose plans
will need to scope down; better they hear it upfront than
discover it 30 minutes in.

#### Planning (10 min)

Have them work through the 4 questions silently. Walk the room.

Specific moves:

- **Kid stuck on question 1 (no idea):** Read the seed list
  with them. Pick one. Modify. Move on. Don't let them spend
  more than 3 minutes deciding.
- **Kid with overscoped plan:** Read it back. "Okay, this is
  ambitious. Question 4 — what's the simplest version? What
  would you build FIRST?" Push hard. The minimum should be
  buildable in 30 minutes.
- **Kid with undersized plan:** Push the other way. "This is
  doable in 10 minutes. What could you ADD if it works?" Have
  them list stretch additions in question 4.
- **Kid who finished planning in 5 minutes:** Read it. Sign off
  if it's solid. Have them start building immediately.

#### Sign-off

Every kid gets a Mr. Eric sign-off before building. Look for:

- Are all 4 questions answered?
- Is question 4 (simplest version) buildable in ~30 min?
- Do the functions in question 2 actually map to things they
  can build with what they know?

If yes, sign off. If no, ask one focused question. Don't
redesign their plan; nudge.

#### Building (~35 min)

Walk the room aggressively. Triage:

- **Working code, polishing:** Let them. They're fine.
- **Mostly working, has a bug:** Sit with them, debug.
- **Has pieces but doesn't run:** Help get to runnable. Cut
  features ruthlessly.
- **Hasn't built much:** Spend the most time here. Help them
  build the simplest possible version even if it's just one
  mountain or one flower or one letter.

**Don't write code on a kid's machine.** Sit with them, ask
"what do you want to happen next?", "what blocks could do that?",
get them to articulate the move. Then they type.

### Teaching Part B

#### Polish + buddy swap

Brief check-in:

> "Five minutes. Whatever you have, we're demoing soon. Make
> sure it runs. Then swap with your buddy and play each other's
> for two minutes."

Walk the room. Help kids whose code crashed at the last minute.
The goal is "it runs" — not "it's great."

#### Demos

Pick a kid you trust to model the format on the first demo.
Walk them through:

- Show the drawing (run it).
- Tell us what it is.
- Tell us one thing that was hard.
- Take one question.

Then your specific praise (~30 sec).

For subsequent demos, enforce the structure but lightly. Be
ready to:

- **Prompt a kid who skips "what was hard":** "Anything tricky?"
- **Generate a question if no one volunteers:** "I was wondering
  about the colors — how did you pick them?"
- **Handle a project that doesn't run:** "Walk us through what
  it should do. Even with the bug, this is a real piece of work
  and we want to see it." Then fold the bug into your praise as
  a real-programmer moment.

**Specific praise. Specific praise. Specific praise.** This is
the most important thing you do today. Modeled praise becomes
class culture. Generic praise → generic culture. Specific praise
→ kids learn to notice details in each other's work.

### The Phase 2 close

After demos, before kids leave, mark the moment:

> "Phase 2 is over. Eight weeks ago, you'd never typed real
> Python. You'd never seen an error message. Today, every one
> of you typed a complete program with functions and loops and
> conditionals, and made the computer draw something you
> imagined. That's the journey from blocks to real code. You
> did it.
>
> "Next week we leave the turtle behind. Phase 3 is Python
> getting general — text, lists, files, debuggers. Less drawing,
> more *thinking with code*. It's a real shift, and you're
> ready for it."

Then a beat. Then "see you next week."

This isn't quite the PB&J callback weight of Phase 1 Session 9 —
that was the curriculum's emotional centerpiece. Today is a
warm send-off, not a high-emotion moment. Calibrate accordingly.

### Common stumbles

- **Plan that's too ambitious.** Most common. Push to question
  4. If they can't simplify, sit with them and help.
- **Plan with no buildable functions.** "I want to draw a
  dragon" without thinking about what functions a dragon
  decomposes into. Help: "What shape is the body? The wings?
  The head? Each could be a function."
- **Built something that crashes mid-demo.** Don't panic. Walk
  them through the design as if it had worked. Specific praise
  for the design they intended.
- **Kid who refuses to demo.** Same as Phase 1: don't force
  public demo. Have them demo to you in the back of the room
  during another kid's transition. They still demoed.
- **Demos run long, you're going to run out of time.** Cut
  praise to 15 sec, make questions optional. Don't cut anyone's
  demo.

### Differentiation

- **Younger kids (9-10):** Their projects will be simpler. Praise
  the *thinking* — the choice of subject, the function
  decomposition. Don't compare to older kids' projects.
- **Older kids (12+):** May have more polished projects. Praise
  *risk-taking* — what they tried that they hadn't done before.
- **Advanced (any age):** May have built something genuinely
  impressive. Celebrate without putting other kids' work in the
  shade.
- **Struggling:** The kid whose project is barely functional
  needs the most careful demo handling. Their experience of
  Phase 2's last day must be "I built something and people
  saw it."

### What to watch for

- **Kids who scope down their ambitious plans during Part A and
  end up with something that works.** This is the lesson
  landing. Note them — they're now better engineers than they
  were at 9am.
- **Kids inspired by other kids' demos.** "I want to do that
  next time" → note for Phase 3's first project.
- **The end-of-phase mood.** If kids leave proud and curious
  about Phase 3, you nailed the close. If they leave feeling
  rushed or frustrated, the timing was too tight — adjust for
  next year (maybe split into 2 sessions).
