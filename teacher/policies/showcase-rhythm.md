# Milestones and showcases

## The distinction

Two different things, often conflated:

- **Milestone project** = capstone of a phase. Every student does
  it. Proves they internalized that phase's material. Part of the
  curriculum, not optional.
- **Showcase** = public-facing event where students demo work to
  family, church, whoever. Less frequent than milestones. Pulls from
  milestone projects plus personal projects.

The distinction matters because milestones run on the curriculum's
clock (end of each phase) and showcases run on a *social* clock (what
fits the church and families). Conflating them locks you into
showcasing every six weeks, which won't work.

## Rhythm

| Event | Frequency | Audience | Length |
|-------|-----------|----------|--------|
| Internal demo day | End of each phase (~6-8/yr) | Class only | ~30-45 min within session |
| Public showcase | Twice yearly (fall, spring) | Families, church | ~90 min standalone event |

Twice yearly maps to how church calendars naturally work, gives
every student at least one showcase moment within ~6 months of
joining, and lets you theme them differently (fall = "what we've
built so far," spring = "year-end celebration").

## Public showcase format

Designed around 9-15 year olds who are nervous about presenting:

- **Stations, not stage.** Each student at a table with their
  machine and project running. Family circulates. Way less terrifying
  than presenting to a room, and the conversations are richer.
- **Project cards.** Each student has a card on their table: project
  name, what it does, what they learned, what was hardest.
  Pre-written. Helps parents who don't know what to ask, and gives
  the student a fallback when nervous.
- **Big-screen rotation.** A projector cycles through 30-second
  highlight clips of each project for ambient atmosphere and for
  grandparents who can't easily walk around.
- **Food.** Non-negotiable. Showcases without food are just exams
  with witnesses.
- **Public recognition.** At wrap-up, you say a few words about each
  student. Public recognition matters; kids remember it.
- **~90 min total:** 15 min setup, 60 min open demo, 15 min wrap-up.

## Internal demo day format

Lower-key, fits inside a regular class session at the end of each
phase:

- Each student demos their milestone project to the class, ~3-5 min
  each.
- Other students ask one question per demo. Builds the habit of
  engaging with others' code.
- You give specific, concrete praise — "I liked how you handled the
  case where the user types something weird." Models the kind of
  feedback you want them giving each other.
- ~30-45 min total, fits in Part B of a session.

This gives the milestone project social weight without requiring a
public event, and it builds the muscles for the public showcase.

## Implications for curriculum design

Knowing what you're designing toward:

- **Every phase ends with a milestone project** that's demoable in
  3-5 minutes and is visually or audibly interesting. "Refactored a
  function" is not a milestone. "Built a quiz game about Bible
  trivia" is.
- **Phase milestones produce artifacts students can keep.** A Pygame
  game they can run on their machine; a customtkinter app icon on
  their desktop; a hosted web page; a Flask app screenshot. The
  artifact matters as much as the skill.
- **Public showcases pull from milestone projects + personal
  projects.** Students choose what to demo. Some pick their best
  milestone; some pick a passion project they did on their own.
  Both are good.
- **Phase length is partly constrained by milestone difficulty.**
  Don't pad. If a phase's milestone realistically needs 12 sessions
  of build-up, fine. If it needs 6, also fine.
