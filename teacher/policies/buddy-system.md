# Buddy system

The mechanics matter more than the concept. Loose pairings die.

## Mechanics

- **Every student has a buddy. You assign them, not the kids.**
  Self-selection breeds cliques and orphans the quiet kid.
- **Mixed-experience pairings by default.** Newer student gets help;
  older student gets the depth that comes from teaching. Same-level
  buddies work fine for kids who joined together, but mixed is the
  default.
- **Reassign every ~8-10 sessions.** Roughly once per phase. Prevents
  stagnation, stops one kid from carrying another forever, and
  exposes everyone to multiple working styles.
- **Buddies sit together in class.** Physical proximity does most of
  the work. They're each other's first line of help before you.
- **The rule: ask your buddy first, then Mr. Eric.** Cuts your
  interrupt rate dramatically. More importantly, it forces students
  to articulate what they're stuck on, which is half of debugging.
  If buddy doesn't know either, *then* it comes to you, and now you
  have two students learning at once.
- **Public mentor recognition for older students buddying well.**
  When a teen or older kid is buddying a younger one well, name it
  out loud. "Caleb has been a great buddy to Jonah this month —
  thanks, Caleb." Public recognition is rocket fuel for older kids
  who'd otherwise be bored.
- **Buddy ≠ permanent.** Make clear up front that buddies rotate, so
  no one feels stuck and no one feels possessive.

## Pairing thoughtfully

Standard youth-ministry instincts apply. Be thoughtful about who you
pair with whom — sibling dynamics, friend-group dynamics, and
especially cross-gender pairings as kids hit middle-school age. Don't
overthink it, but don't be careless either.

## What this is downstream of

The buddy system is one of the highest-leverage moves available to
you in a single-teacher, mixed-age, mixed-skill room. It serves the
"older students mentoring younger ones" principle from
`CURRICULUM-DECISIONS.md`, and it serves the goal-3 students (future
programmers) by giving them an early experience of teaching, which
is one of the best ways to deepen their own understanding.
