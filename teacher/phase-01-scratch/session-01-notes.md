## Session 1 — Teacher Notes

*Phase 1, Scratch · Session 1 of 9 · Title: Welcome & the peanut
butter problem*

### Purpose of this session

This session is doing four things at once, in order of importance:

1. **Setting class culture for the entire year.** Computers are dumb
   and literal. Precision matters. Being wrong is normal. Laughing
   is fine. This is your one chance to plant these flags before any
   actual material competes for attention. Don't rush past it.
2. **Building rapport.** Day one of any class is awkward. PB&J is a
   great icebreaker because the kids get to laugh *at the teacher*,
   which immediately lowers the stakes for them.
3. **Reading the room.** This is your first chance to see who's
   confident, who's tentative, who's already done some Scratch, who's
   never used a computer with a real keyboard. Don't assign buddies
   yet — just observe.
4. **Getting Scratch open and clicked-around.** A distant fourth. If
   you only get through PB&J today, you've still set up the entire
   year successfully. Part B is a bonus.

### Before class

**Bring:**

- Loaf of bread (sliced)
- Jar of peanut butter
- Jar of jelly
- Butter knife
- A plate
- Napkins / paper towels
- A comb, or some other absurd "spreading tool" — a spatula, a ruler,
  a Sharpie. Pick something that's clearly *not* a knife. The visual
  gag is half the lesson.
- A trash bag for cleanup

**Set up:**

- All machines on, Scratch icon visible on desktop. (You imaged for
  this — just verify.)
- Projector if you have one, mirroring your screen for the Scratch
  tour. Not required but nicer than crowding around one machine.
- Have a backup plan if Scratch fails to launch on someone's machine
  — a buddy machine they can share for today.
- Open Scratch on your own machine before class starts so you can
  demo without fumbling.

**Prep time:** ~20 minutes including grocery run.

### Timing and flow

Total: ~90 min. Two halves with a hard break, per the cross-cutting
structure.

- **Welcome and intro** (~5 min) — names, what this class is, why
  we're here.
- **Part A: PB&J and discussion** (~40 min) — the demo runs ~20 min,
  discussion ~10-15 min, wrap-up framing ~5 min.
- **Break** (~5 min) — bathroom, water, snack.
- **Part B: Scratch tour and free play** (~35 min) — tour ~10 min,
  guided exercise ~5 min, free play ~15-20 min, wrap-up ~5 min.

If running short, **Part B is the cuttable half.** Part A is the
session. Don't compromise on it.

### Teaching Part A — PB&J

This bit only works if you commit to the bit. The kids need to see
you take instructions seriously and then execute them in deadpan,
willfully literal ways. The more you ham it up while staying earnest
about "doing what they said," the harder they laugh and the deeper
the lesson lands.

Some specific moments to engineer:

- **"Put the peanut butter on the bread"** → place the unopened jar
  on the unopened bag. Stare blankly. "Done. What's next?"
- **"Open the peanut butter"** → wait for them to specify how. If
  they say "with your hands," try (and fail) to unscrew it without
  using your fingers, only your palms. If they say "unscrew it," ask
  which direction. Lean into it.
- **"Spread the peanut butter on the bread"** → with what? When they
  say "with the knife," ask which side. When they say "the flat
  side," do it — but the bread is still in the bag. Spread peanut
  butter on the outside of the bag. Let them notice.
- **The comb moment.** At some point a student will say something
  like "use the spreading thing." Pull out the comb. "This?" Spread
  peanut butter through the comb's teeth onto the bag. They will
  lose it. This is the high point. Save it.
- **Be patient.** Let students figure out their own corrections.
  Don't bail them out by inferring intent. The whole point is that
  you *can't* infer intent.

When the demo is done (or the energy is starting to flag, whichever
comes first), gather them and frame:

> "This is what programming is. The computer is me, but worse — at
> least I know what bread is. The computer doesn't even know that.
> Your job, all year, is going to be learning how to give
> instructions so precise that even something as dumb as a computer
> can follow them. And here's the cool part: when you can do that,
> you can make the computer do *anything*."

That's the line. Land it cleanly. It's the thesis statement for the
entire curriculum, and you'll refer back to it constantly.

### Teaching Part B — Scratch tour

Goal: comfort, not mastery. You want every student to leave able to
(1) find Scratch on their machine, (2) drag a block, (3) click it
and see something happen. Anything beyond that is gravy.

During the tour, resist the urge to teach extra concepts. Don't
explain what loops are because someone asks. Don't get into sprites'
costumes. Don't show the sound editor. Each of those is a future
session.

During free play, walk the room. Don't help unless asked. Look for:

- **Confident kids who are already trying to make something
  specific.** These are likely candidates to buddy a newer kid in a
  few sessions. Note who they are.
- **Tentative kids who are afraid to click anything.** Reassure
  explicitly: "You can't break Scratch. Click everything." If a kid
  is still frozen after that, sit with them for a minute and click
  one thing yourself, then hand it back.
- **Kids who finish the guided exercise in 30 seconds.** Suggest a
  stretch goal. "Try to make the cat walk in a square."
- **Kids with prior Scratch experience.** Don't make them do the
  tour over again — let them help a neighbor, or let them just play.

### Common stumbles

- **"What if I break it?"** Most common day-one anxiety. Reassure
  firmly. Software can't be broken by clicking.
- **Mouse vs. trackpad confusion.** The W202NA trackpads are
  mediocre. Encourage external mice if available; if not, just be
  patient — drag-and-drop on a small trackpad is genuinely hard for
  small hands.
- **Kid who tries to do everything by typing.** Some kids assume
  programming means typing. Gently redirect: "In Scratch, we drag.
  Typing comes later in the year."
- **Kid who's used Scratch and is bored.** Pair them with a buddy
  who's new and frame it as "you're helping teach." Mentor framing
  established on day one.
- **Kid who can't see the cat because their stage area is
  collapsed.** Easy fix — show them the layout toggle in the top
  right. Don't stress.

### Differentiation

- **Younger kids (9-10):** May need help finding blocks by color
  when they're not next to you. Encourage their buddy to help (when
  buddies are assigned) or check on them during free play.
- **Older kids (12+):** May find PB&J childish. They won't. Trust
  the bit. Older kids laugh harder, in fact, because they get the
  meta-joke. If one is genuinely too cool, give them a more
  open-ended Scratch challenge during Part B.
- **Kids with prior experience:** Don't hold them back. Let them
  explore freely. Note their level for buddy assignments.

### What to watch for

- Kids who seem disengaged in Part A. PB&J should engage everyone;
  if it's not landing for one kid, something else is going on
  (nervous, hungry, doesn't know anyone). Make a note, follow up
  gently next week.
- Buddy candidates. Don't assign yet, but note who would pair well
  with whom. Mixed-experience pairings work best; mixed-confidence
  pairings can also work.
- Names. Memorize them this week. Day-one name recall is huge for
  trust.
- Anyone whose parent stayed and is hovering. Gently make sure they
  know they don't need to stay; some will want to.

### Connections forward

- The PB&J framing will be referenced constantly. Anytime a student
  gets frustrated that their code "doesn't work" — peanut butter
  callback. Anytime someone makes an unstated assumption — peanut
  butter callback.
- Bring the bread back at the **end of Phase 1** (Session 9, demo
  day) for a 5-minute callback: "Remember when we couldn't even tell
  me how to make a sandwich? Look what you can tell a computer to do
  now." Visceral progress moment.
- Bring it back **again at the start of Phase 3** (Session 1 of
  Python), when syntax is about to bite for the first time.
  "Remember why precision matters? Now we're going to learn what the
  computer says when you're imprecise."
