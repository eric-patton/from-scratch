## Session 9 — Teacher Notes

*Phase 1, Scratch · Session 9 of 9 · Title: Milestone project work
day 2 + demo day*

### Purpose of this session

Last day of Phase 1. Five jobs, in priority order:

1. **Make absolutely sure every kid demos something.** A kid who
   leaves Phase 1 without showing the room a thing they built will
   carry that quietly for a long time. Even if their game is
   trivial, it gets demoed. No exceptions.
2. **Run the peanut butter callback.** This is the planned moment
   from Session 1. The contrast between "you couldn't tell me how
   to make a sandwich" and "you built a game and demoed it" is the
   emotional close of the phase. Land it.
3. **Model demo-and-question culture.** This is the first internal
   demo day. The way you do it sets the tone for every demo day
   after (one per phase + the public showcases). Specific, kind,
   concrete praise. Specific, kind, concrete questions.
4. **End the phase warmly.** Phase 2 starts next week with
   typing-not-dragging — a real shift. End Phase 1 on a celebration
   so the energy carries into the harder work ahead.
5. **Note next-phase signals.** Watch the kids during their demos.
   Who has natural product instincts? Who's a precise builder? Who
   thinks about the user? Each of those is data for Phase 2 buddy
   assignments.

### Before class

**Bring:**

- Snacks if you can swing it. Demo days deserve food.
- Class roster with two columns: project name + one specific
  praise note per kid (you'll fill in praise during their demos).
- Optional: a small "you finished Phase 1" card or sticker for
  each kid. Not required, but the gesture matters.

**Set up:**

- Demo machine free (kids will demo on their own machines).
- Project files: make sure each kid's project is on the machine
  they'll demo on. If a kid has been working on a different
  machine, pre-load their file before class.
- Kids' chairs/spots arranged so everyone can see whoever's
  demoing. A semicircle around one machine works well.

**Prep time:** ~15 minutes. The setup is light; the prep is
*reviewing your Session 8 notes* about each kid's project status,
so you know who needs help finishing and who's ready to polish.

### Timing and flow

Total: ~90 min. The structure is unusual — Part A is shorter, Part
B is longer.

- **Welcome and frame** (~5 min) — what today is, "let's finish,
  then demo."
- **Part A: final polish + buddy testing** (~30 min) — build/fix
  ~20 min, buddy swap ~10 min.
- **Break** (~5 min) — also reset the room into demo configuration
  if needed.
- **Part B: demos** (~45 min) — assume 8 students × 5 min each =
  40 min, plus 5 min for transitions.
- **Wrap-up + phase close** (~5 min) — Mr. Eric's per-kid
  comments, peanut butter callback, "next week is Python."

If running short, **demos take priority over polish.** Better to
demo a game with one bug than to skip demos.

If you have more or fewer than 8 kids, scale demo time:
- 6 kids: 6 min each.
- 10 kids: 4 min each (push hard on time).
- 12 kids: split into two demo halves with a break, or 3-4 min
  each.

### Teaching Part A

This is the "you're almost done — let's land this" half.

- Open with: "Today we finish, then we show. The first half is
  fixing whatever's not quite right. The second half is demos.
  No new content."
- Walk the room aggressively. Check on every kid in the first 10
  minutes. Triage:
  - **Working game, polishing:** Let them. They're fine.
  - **Mostly working, has one bug:** Sit with them, help debug.
  - **Has pieces but doesn't run:** Help them get to a runnable
    state. Cut features ruthlessly if needed.
  - **Hasn't built much:** This is the kid you spend the most time
    with. Help them build the simplest possible version of their
    plan, even if it's just "sprite moves and there's a score." A
    minimal game is better than no game.
- **The 15-minute mark is the buddy-swap signal.** Pause the room.
  "Okay, swap. Play your buddy's game for two minutes. Then tell
  them one thing." Walk during this — listen for what kids notice
  about each other's games. Often illuminating.
- **The 30-minute mark** is your transition into demos. Even if
  some kids aren't quite ready, draw the line. Better to demo a
  imperfect game than to skip demos.

The peanut butter callback timing: **save it for the wrap-up,
after demos.** Don't use it at the start. The contrast between
"couldn't make a sandwich" and "just demoed a game" lands harder
*after* they've shown what they built.

### Teaching Part B (demos)

The first demo sets the tone. **Pick a kid you trust to model
the format.** Ideally one with a working game and reasonable
confidence. Walk through what a demo looks like with them:

- They show their game (~1-2 min).
- They tell us about it (~1 min).
- They tell us one thing that was hard (~30 sec).
- One person from the room asks one question (~30 sec).
- You give specific praise (~30 sec).

For the rest of the demos, **enforce the structure but lightly.**
If a kid skips "what was hard," prompt them: "Was anything tricky
to figure out?" If a question doesn't materialize, you ask one
yourself.

#### Specific praise — the most important thing you do today

When you give praise after each demo, **be specific and concrete.**

- Bad: "Great job! That was awesome."
- Good: "I really liked how you made the rocks fall at different
  speeds — that made the game way harder to predict."

The kids will mirror your praise style for each other in future
sessions. Generic praise → kids learn to give generic praise.
Specific praise → they learn to *notice* specific things in each
other's work, which is the goal.

If you can't think of something specific for a kid, you weren't
paying attention during their demo. (No judgment — it happens. In
that case, ask them a question that lets them tell you something
specific: "what was your favorite part of building this?")

#### Handling the kid whose game doesn't quite work

It will happen. The kid clicks the green flag, the game breaks,
they look mortified.

Move fast:

- "Oh, that's a bug. Can you tell us what *should* have happened?"
- They explain the intended behavior.
- "Got it. Even with the bug, this is a real game, and I want
  everyone to see what you were going for. Walk us through what
  the design is."

Then they describe their game even though it doesn't run. **They
still demoed.** They still get specific praise. The bug is part of
the story; embarrassment is not.

This is also the moment to model the "peanut butter problem"
publicly: "what was the computer doing when you wanted it to do
something else? That's the precision lesson from week 1." The bug
becomes a learning moment for the room.

### The peanut butter callback

Save this for the very end, after all demos and your closing
remarks. Roughly:

> "I want you to remember something. The very first day of class,
> back in Session 1, I pretended to be a computer and you had to
> tell me how to make a peanut butter and jelly sandwich. You
> couldn't do it. I put the jar on the bag. I tried to spread
> peanut butter with a comb. We laughed for half an hour because
> nobody could give me precise enough instructions to make a
> sandwich.
>
> "That was eight weeks ago.
>
> "Today, you each gave instructions to a computer — which is way
> stupider than I am, by the way — precise enough that it ran a
> *game*. Not a sandwich. A game. With sprites and scores and
> falling apples.
>
> "That's a pretty big deal. Take a second and feel that."

Then a beat of silence. Then "Okay, see you next week, we're going
to Python."

The kids will remember this moment. It's worth the 90 seconds.

### Common stumbles

- **Kid wants to add features instead of fixing bugs in Part A.**
  Redirect: "what's broken right now? Fix that first." Features
  can wait; broken games can't be demoed.
- **Two kids' game files got mixed up on shared machines.** Rare
  but possible if you're using a shared computer setup. Have a
  process: each kid saves with their name in the filename.
- **A kid refuses to demo.** Rare but happens, usually with a kid
  who's quiet or anxious. Don't force a public demo. Have them
  demo just to you in the back of the room while others demo to
  the group. They still demoed. They still get praise.
- **Demos run long, you're going to run out of time.** Cut your
  per-demo praise to 10 seconds and the question to optional.
  Don't cut anyone's actual demo.
- **A kid's game crashes during demo.** See "handling the kid
  whose game doesn't quite work" above. Treat it as a learning
  moment, not a failure.

### Differentiation

- **Younger kids (9-10):** Their games may be simpler. That's
  fine. Praise the *thinking* — "I love how you decided the cat
  could only catch one type of food." Don't compare to older kids'
  games.
- **Older kids (12+):** Their games may be much more polished.
  Praise the *risk-taking* — "you tried something I haven't seen
  before, with the level system." Encourage but don't over-elevate
  vs. younger kids' work.
- **Advanced (any age) with prior programming:** May have built
  something genuinely impressive. Same advice — celebrate without
  putting other kids' work in the shade. The room is mixed-skill;
  comparison is poison.
- **Struggling:** A kid whose game is barely functional needs the
  most careful demo handling. Make sure they get demo time. Make
  sure they get specific praise. Make sure their experience of
  Phase 1's last day is "I built something and people saw it."

### What to watch for

- **Kids' reactions to others' games.** Some will be surprised.
  Some will get jealous. Some will feel inspired. All useful info
  for the next phase.
- **The kid who says "I want to do that next time."** Note them.
  Phase 2's first project should give them a chance to try.
- **Buddy dynamics.** Did buddies actually play each other's games
  and give useful feedback? If yes, the buddy system is working.
  If no, time to reassign.
- **The end-of-phase mood.** If kids leave excited, you nailed it.
  If kids leave deflated, something went sideways and it's worth
  reflecting on what.

### Connections forward

- **Phase 2 (Python with Turtle).** Next week's session is the
  start of typing-not-dragging. The peanut-butter callback today
  sets up the *next* peanut-butter moment in Phase 3 Session 1
  (when syntax errors first hit). Acknowledge in Session 2-1 that
  the transition feels different.
- **Public showcases (twice yearly).** Today's demos are practice.
  By the time the first public showcase rolls around (probably
  mid-Phase 3), kids have done several internal demo days and
  know the format.
- **Buddy reassignments before Phase 2.** End of phase = buddy
  rotation moment per the buddy system policy. Use what you saw
  today (especially in Part A's swap) to inform new pairings.
- **Personal projects from today.** Some kids will keep working on
  their milestone project at home over the next week. Encourage
  it. This is the goal-2 hook (programming as a tool for *their*
  things) starting to take root.
