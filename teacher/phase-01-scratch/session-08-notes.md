## Session 8 — Teacher Notes

*Phase 1, Scratch · Session 8 of 9 · Title: Milestone project work
day 1*

### Purpose of this session

This session is structurally different from 1-7. There's no new
concept. You're a facilitator, not a lecturer. Four jobs, in
priority order:

1. **Force every student to do the planning step.** Most kids
   want to skip planning and just start building. Don't let them.
   Planning is goal #1 of the curriculum (decomposition) made
   concrete. Five minutes of planning saves an hour of flailing.
2. **Help every student scope down to a buildable thing.** Some
   kids will arrive with wildly ambitious ideas. Your job is to
   help them find the *simplest version that's still fun* — the
   thing they can actually finish in two sessions. Push back
   gently but firmly on scope creep.
3. **Set up Session 9's success.** Every kid needs to leave today
   with at least *something* working — even a moving sprite is
   enough. The kid who leaves with nothing is the kid who shows
   up next week and says "I don't have a project."
4. **Make sure every kid has at least one idea.** Some will arrive
   with multiple, some with none. The "starting points" list
   exists for the latter group.

### Before class

**Bring:**

- A printed copy of the seven planning questions for any kid who
  didn't bring paper (one sheet per student is fine).
- A pen.
- Optional: a printed list of the "starting points" (catch game
  variations, etc.) for kids without ideas.

**Set up:**

- Demo machine ready to show students the planning page from the
  textbook if they ask, plus the Apples and Rocks game from
  Session 7 as a reference.
- Class roster with each student's name and (if you noted it from
  Session 7) any milestone idea they mentioned.

**Prep time:** ~10 minutes including printing. Plus: spend 10
minutes the night before reviewing your Session 7 notes about
which kids mentioned which ideas. You'll be more helpful in Part
A's planning if you can say "you mentioned a maze game last week
— still want to do that?"

### Timing and flow

Total: ~90 min, two halves with a softer break than usual.

- **Welcome and intro** (~5 min) — what today is, what next week
  is, frame the planning step as "the most important thing."
- **Part A: planning** (~35 min) — silent planning ~15 min,
  buddy review ~5 min, Mr. Eric sign-off ~10 min (overlaps with
  buddy review since you can sign off as kids finish), buffer ~5
  min.
- **Break** (~5 min).
- **Part B: build** (~40 min) — pure build time, you circulate.
- **Wrap-up** (~5 min) — "one thing I got working" round-robin.

If running short, **the end-of-session sharing can be cut.** It's
nice but not essential.

### Teaching Part A

The frame matters. Open with something like:

> "Today we're going to do something that doesn't *feel* like
> programming, but is actually the most important part of any
> programming project. We're going to plan. The reason real software
> projects fail isn't usually because the code is hard — it's
> because nobody decided what they were building first. Today, we
> decide."

Then have them work through the seven questions silently for ~15
minutes. **Walk the room while they're writing.** Read over
shoulders. Make notes about who's writing fast (probably has a
clear idea), who's stuck on question 1 (probably has no idea), who
wrote a paragraph for each question (probably overscoping).

Specific moves:

- **For the kid stuck on question 1 (no idea):** Sit with them.
  Read the starting-points list together. Say "what feels close
  to fun for you?" Pick one. Modify it slightly. Move on. The
  perfect idea is the enemy of the shippable game.
- **For the kid writing a paragraph for each question
  (overscoping):** Read theirs back to them. "Okay, this is
  ambitious. Question 7 — what's the simplest version that's still
  fun? What would you build *first*?" Push hard on this. Better
  to ship a tiny game next week than to abandon a big one.
- **For the kid who finished in 5 minutes (probably underscoping
  or just confident):** Read theirs. If they have a buildable
  plan, sign off and let them start. If they're missing
  something obvious (no game over condition, for example), ask
  about it.

The buddy-review piece (5 min) is a chance for kids to catch
their own mistakes by explaining the plan out loud. Don't let
buddies redesign each other's games — keep it to "one
question/comment each."

The Mr. Eric sign-off is a real review, not a rubber stamp. Look
at:

- Is the plan buildable in ~90 minutes (one Part B + most of next
  week's session)?
- Are all seven questions actually answered?
- Does question 7 (simplest version) describe something the kid
  could build *today*?

If yes to all three, sign off. If no, ask one focused question.
Don't redesign their game; nudge them toward fixing the gap.

### Teaching Part B

This is just build time. Your job is **walking the room and
unsticking individuals.** Some specifics:

- **Don't write code on a kid's machine.** If they're stuck, ask
  them what they want to happen next. Then ask them what blocks
  they think might do that. Get them to say it. Then they type it.
  Resist the urge to take the keyboard.
- **Use the textbook.** If a kid forgot how to do smooth movement,
  point them at Session 5. If they forgot how to make a variable,
  Session 6. The textbook is the reference; using it is a skill
  worth building.
- **Pair kids who hit similar walls.** "Maya is also trying to do
  collisions — go talk to her." Buddy collaboration is huge here.
- **Note kids who are flying through.** They're candidates for
  helping others, OR for adding to their own project's stretch
  ideas. Engage them.
- **Note kids who are stalled.** Some kids will make a beautiful
  plan and then not know where to start coding. Sit with them.
  Open a new project. Drag the first block. Say "okay, you tell
  me what's next." Get them moving by removing the blank-page
  problem.

The wrap-up (one thing I got working) serves two purposes:

- It celebrates progress, however small.
- It surfaces who didn't get *anything* working — which is who you
  need to follow up with before next week's session, even if just
  via a "hey, want help getting started before class next week?"
  message.

### Common stumbles

- **No idea for a project.** Most common. Fix: starting-points
  list, sit with them, pick one, modify slightly.
- **Way too ambitious idea ("I want to make Minecraft").** Common
  among older kids who underestimate scope. Fix: question 7,
  hard. Force the simplest version.
- **Plans that don't actually have a game in them ("the cat just
  walks around").** Missing goal/win/lose. Ask: "how does the
  player know they did well?" If they can't answer, that's the
  next thing to add.
- **Plan with no implementation path ("the dragon casts spells").**
  Cool idea but how? Walk through how each piece would work in
  Scratch using what they know. If a piece doesn't have a
  Scratch-blocks answer, simplify it.
- **Frozen at the keyboard.** They have a plan, they don't know
  where to start typing. Sit with them, drag the first block
  yourself, hand back the keyboard.
- **Reuses Apples-and-Rocks code wholesale and calls it their
  project.** Gentle pushback: that's a great starting point, but
  what's *different* about your version? Push for at least one
  meaningful change.

### Differentiation

- **Younger kids (9-10):** Will need more help with planning.
  Expect to spend more time with them. The starting-points list
  is your friend; small modifications to a known shape are
  easier than blank-page invention. Don't make them invent —
  let them remix.
- **Older kids (12+):** Will overscope. That's the failure mode.
  Push them on question 7 hard. The kid who wants to build "an
  RPG with three bosses and a story" needs to leave class today
  having built "a player who walks around and one boss they can
  fight."
- **Advanced (any age):** May actually be able to scope something
  ambitious within reason. Trust them more — but ask "what's the
  riskiest part of this?" and have them build that part *first*,
  not last. If they can't build the risky part, the rest is
  pointless.
- **Struggling:** A kid who can't reproduce Session 7's basic
  cat-with-arrow-keys is the kid you watch most carefully today.
  Their milestone may need to be "build something close to Apples
  and Rocks but with different sprites." That's a totally valid
  outcome — better to ship something based on a known shape than
  to attempt something they can't finish.

### What to watch for

- The kid who writes the perfect plan and then can't start
  building. Common. Sit with them, drag the first block, hand
  back keyboard.
- The kid who's been silent. Some kids work quietly and are
  fine; some are silently frustrated. Check in mid-Part-B with
  every kid you haven't seen progress from in 20 minutes.
- The kid whose buddy is more advanced. Sometimes the more
  advanced buddy will silently take over the project. Watch for
  this; gently redirect ("Caleb, your job is to help Sam do
  this, not to do it yourself").
- Excitement levels. Most kids will be visibly invested in their
  own project in a way they weren't for the guided builds. Lean
  into that energy.

### Connections forward

- **Session 9 (next week, milestone day 2 + demo day).** Today's
  build sets up next week's polish + demo. The kids who left
  today with something working are in good shape; the ones who
  didn't need extra support before/early in next week's class.
- **Phase 2 (Python Turtle).** The planning habit introduced today
  carries forward — every project in every later phase will start
  with "what are we building?" before code.
- **Pygame milestone (Phase 6).** Same project shape — plan, scope
  down, build the simplest thing first, iterate. Today is the
  first run-through of the "ship a project" cycle they'll repeat
  several times across the curriculum.
- **The "perfect idea is the enemy of the shippable game" lesson
  is itself a callback opportunity** every time a kid hits scope
  trouble in any future project.
